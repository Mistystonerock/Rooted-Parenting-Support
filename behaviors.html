Anger Outbursts

What it may mean

A child may feel overwhelmed, unsafe, or unable to name big feelings. Anger can be the body’s way of showing distress when words are not available yet.

What to say

  • I can see this feels really big right now.
  • You are having a hard time. I am here with you.
  • Let’s work on getting your body calm first.

What to avoid

  • Calm down right now.
  • You are being bad.
  • Stop acting crazy.

Calming steps

  • Lower your voice and reduce words.
  • Move to a quieter space if possible.
  • Offer water, breathing, or a sensory tool.

Anxiety / Worry

What is happening

The child's brain is trying to stay safe. Their body may feel tense or overwhelmed.

What to say

  • "You are safe right now."
  • "We can take this one step at a time."
  • "I believe in you."

Support steps

  • slow breathing together
  • break task into small steps
  • offer reassurance

Tantrums / Emotional Overload

What is happening

The child is overwhelmed and does not yet have the skills to regulate emotions. Their nervous system is in distress mode.

What to say

  • "I can see this is really hard right now."
  • "You're not in trouble. I'm here to help."
  • "We can figure this out together."

Avoid saying

  • "Stop crying right now."
  • "You're being dramatic."
  • "If you don't stop..."

Support steps

  • Stay calm.
  • Reduce stimulation.
  • Offer presence without pressure.
  • Talk after child is calm.

Shutdown / Withdrawal

What is happening

Child feels overwhelmed and disconnects as a protection response.

What to say

  • "I'm here when you're ready."
  • "You don't have to talk yet."

Support steps

  • reduce pressure
  • offer quiet presence
  • allow processing time

Defiance / Not Listening

What is happening

The child may feel powerless, unheard, or overwhelmed. Control can feel like safety.

What to say

  • "Let's figure this out together."
  • "You have two choices."
  • "I will help you succeed."

Avoid saying

  • "Because I said so."
  • "You never listen."

Support steps

  • Give simple choices.
  • Use calm voice.
  • Break task into steps.

Lying / Hiding Things

What is happening

Lying often happens when children fear punishment or feel unsafe telling the truth.

What to say

  • "You can tell me the truth. You are safe."
  • "Mistakes help us learn."

Avoid saying

  • "You're a liar."
  • "I can't trust you."

Support steps

  • Reduce shame.
  • Praise honesty.
  • Focus on problem solving.

Aggression / Hitting

What is happening

The child does not yet have safe ways to express anger or frustration.

What to say

  • "I will not let you hurt others."
  • "You are safe. I am here."
  • "Let's find a safer way."

Support steps

  • Block unsafe behavior calmly.
  • Offer safe alternatives.
  • Teach feeling words.

Disrespectful Language

What it may mean

A child may be overwhelmed, reactive, ashamed, or lacking safe words for frustration, disappointment, or hurt.

What to say

  • I can help when words are respectful.
  • I hear that you are upset.
  • You can be mad and still speak safely.

What to avoid

  • You are so rude.
  • Watch your mouth.
  • You are acting like a brat.

Calming steps

  • Do not match the child’s tone.
  • Use one calm limit and step back from arguing.
  • Process the words later when the child is calmer.

Refusing School

What is happening

Child may feel unsafe, overwhelmed, or worried about expectations.

What to say

  • "We will figure this out together."
  • "You don't have to handle this alone."

Support steps

  • identify stressor
  • talk with school supports
  • create gradual plan

Homework Conflict

What it may mean

Homework conflict can reflect stress, frustration, exhaustion, learning difficulty, perfectionism, or a need for more support and structure.

What to say

  • Homework feels hard right now.
  • Let’s start with one small part.
  • I will help you make a plan.

What to avoid

  • This should be easy.
  • You are just being lazy.
  • Why do you always make this such a battle?

Calming steps

  • Start with one small task instead of the whole assignment.
  • Use a timer for work and break cycles.
  • Check if hunger, fatigue, or overload are part of the struggle.

Attention Seeking

What is happening

Connection need is not being met in a positive way.

What to say

  • "I see you."
  • "Let's spend time together."

Support steps

  • schedule connection time
  • notice positive behavior
  • reduce negative attention cycles
