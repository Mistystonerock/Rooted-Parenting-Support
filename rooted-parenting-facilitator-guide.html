Rooted Parenting Facilitator Guide

Trauma-Informed Support for Families

A trauma-informed group guide for CHWs, peer supporters, family support workers, school staff, and parent group facilitators.

How to Use This Guide

This guide turns the 10 Rooted Parenting lessons into a teachable group format. Each lesson is designed to be practical, supportive, and easy to adapt for parent groups, school-based family sessions, peer-led circles, and community workshops.

Who this program is for

  • Parents and caregivers of children with behavioral and emotional challenges
  • Families affected by trauma, chronic stress, anxiety, aggression, shutdown, or school stress
  • Community-based parent groups, school family programs, and caregiver support programs

What facilitators should focus on

  • Helping caregivers understand behavior through a trauma-informed lens
  • Supporting reflection, calm response skills, and realistic next steps
  • Encouraging progress through practice rather than perfection

How to keep sessions safe, supportive, and nonjudgmental

  • Use respectful, non-shaming language throughout the group.
  • Invite participation without pressure to disclose personal trauma details.
  • Normalize that both children and caregivers may be carrying stress.
  • Focus on strengths, skill-building, and reflection instead of blame.

Facilitator notes

  • Use a calm, non-judgmental tone throughout the group.
  • Normalize that caregivers may be carrying their own stress and overwhelm.
  • Focus on reflection, skill-building, and small next steps rather than perfection.
  • Invite discussion, but do not pressure participants to share personal details.

Recommended session length

45 to 60 minutes per session

Suggested group guidelines

  • Respect each person’s story and privacy.
  • Listen without judgment or advice-giving unless invited.
  • Take care of yourself and step out if needed.
  • Focus on learning, reflection, and practical support.

Materials needed

  • Printed lesson pages or digital display
  • Pens and paper for reflection
  • Printable Rooted Parenting worksheets
  • A calm meeting space with chairs and water if possible

Lesson 1: Understanding Behavior as Communication

Objective: Help caregivers view behavior as communication rather than simply misbehavior.

Key teaching points

  • Children often show stress through behavior before they can explain it with words.
  • Big reactions may point to fear, overwhelm, shame, or unmet needs.
  • Curiosity supports connection and problem-solving better than labels.

Group discussion questions

  • What behaviors are hardest for caregivers to interpret calmly?
  • How might parenting responses change when behavior is viewed as communication?

Simple activity

Ask participants to choose one recent behavior and identify two possible feelings or needs underneath it.

Take-home practice

Pause once a day and ask: “What might my child be feeling or needing right now?”

Closing reflection

What is one behavior I want to look at with more curiosity this week?

Lesson 2: How Trauma Impacts the Brain

Objective: Help caregivers understand how trauma and chronic stress shape attention, reactions, and behavior.

Key teaching points

  • Trauma can keep a child’s brain and body on alert.
  • Survival responses can override listening, flexibility, and problem-solving.
  • A calm adult can help a child’s brain feel safer.

Group discussion questions

  • What behaviors make more sense when you think about stress instead of choice?
  • What parts of the day seem to place children most on alert?

Simple activity

Map one daily stress point and discuss what might help make it feel safer or more predictable.

Take-home practice

Choose one stressful time of day and try one calming routine change.

Closing reflection

What changed for me when I thought about stress instead of willful behavior?

Lesson 3: Regulation Before Discipline

Objective: Help caregivers understand why calm bodies learn better and why timing matters during correction.

Key teaching points

  • Children learn best when they are regulated enough to think.
  • Safety and co-regulation often need to come before teaching.
  • Limits still matter, but discipline is more effective after calm returns.

Group discussion questions

  • What usually happens when adults try to teach during a meltdown?
  • What helps caregivers remember to slow down first?

Simple activity

Practice one short calming phrase with the group, such as “We will calm first.”

Take-home practice

Use one regulation-first phrase during a hard moment this week.

Closing reflection

What helps me remember that calm comes before teaching?

Lesson 4: Connection Before Correction

Objective: Help caregivers use connection to reduce defensiveness and support learning.

Key teaching points

  • Connection lowers threat and helps children become more receptive.
  • Connection is not the same as giving in.
  • Repair supports trust after hard moments.

Group discussion questions

  • How do children respond when adults lead with connection first?
  • What are simple ways to connect before correcting?

Simple activity

Have participants rewrite one correction statement as a connection-first statement.

Take-home practice

Use one connecting phrase before correction this week.

Closing reflection

How might connection change the tone of a difficult moment in my home?

Lesson 5: Understanding Triggers

Objective: Help caregivers notice patterns that activate stress responses in children.

Key teaching points

  • Triggers can be sounds, transitions, tones of voice, places, or stressors.
  • Patterns matter more than isolated incidents.
  • Tracking triggers helps adults plan support ahead of time.

Group discussion questions

  • What repeated situations seem to trigger the strongest reactions?
  • What patterns do caregivers notice but often overlook in the moment?

Simple activity

Use a sample trigger tracker and identify what happened before one recent hard moment.

Take-home practice

Track one repeated challenge during the week and note what happened right before it.

Closing reflection

What trigger pattern do I want to pay closer attention to this week?

Lesson 6: Responding to Anger and Defiance

Objective: Help caregivers reduce power struggles and respond with calm, clear support.

Key teaching points

  • Anger and defiance often reflect overwhelm, powerlessness, or misunderstanding.
  • Calm limits help more than escalating back-and-forth.
  • Simple choices can reduce struggle and increase cooperation.

Group discussion questions

  • What tends to turn a hard moment into a power struggle?
  • How can adults stay calm while still holding a limit?

Simple activity

Practice turning one demand into a calm two-choice statement.

Take-home practice

Use one respectful choice phrase during a hard moment this week.

Closing reflection

What helps me stay steady when my child is angry or pushing back?

Lesson 7: Responding to Anxiety and Shutdown

Objective: Help caregivers support children whose distress shows up as worry, panic, silence, or withdrawal.

Key teaching points

  • Not all distress looks loud; some children respond by shutting down.
  • Gentle support, less pressure, and more time can help children feel safer.
  • Calm presence can be more helpful than repeated questioning.

Group discussion questions

  • How do adults usually respond to silence, worry, or withdrawal?
  • What helps a child feel safer when they are overwhelmed?

Simple activity

Compare a high-pressure response with a low-pressure supportive response and discuss the difference.

Take-home practice

Reduce questions once during a shutdown or anxiety moment and offer one simple next step instead.

Closing reflection

How can I offer support with less pressure this week?

Lesson 8: Supporting Children at School

Objective: Help caregivers prepare for school-based support and strengthen family-school collaboration.

Key teaching points

  • School stress can show up as refusal, anger, avoidance, physical complaints, or exhaustion.
  • Specific and realistic supports are often more effective than vague plans.
  • Documentation helps caregivers advocate clearly.

Group discussion questions

  • What part of school is hardest for families to navigate?
  • What school supports have been helpful or hard to access?

Simple activity

Draft one school support request or identify one adult support person a child could check in with.

Take-home practice

Write down one school concern and one support to ask about this week.

Closing reflection

What school support could make the biggest difference for my child right now?

Lesson 9: Parent Self Regulation

Objective: Help caregivers notice their own stress signals and use simple regulation tools during hard moments.

Key teaching points

  • A caregiver’s nervous system affects the moment.
  • Small pauses can shift the tone of an interaction.
  • Self-regulation is a skill to practice, not a standard of perfection.

Group discussion questions

  • What are early signs that a caregiver is becoming overwhelmed?
  • What grounding tools feel realistic in everyday parenting moments?

Simple activity

Guide the group through a one-minute breathing or grounding practice.

Take-home practice

Choose one self-regulation tool to practice before the next stressful moment.

Closing reflection

What are my earliest signs that I need a pause or grounding step?

Lesson 10: Building Long-Term Emotional Safety

Objective: Help caregivers focus on consistency, repair, and everyday safety as the foundation for change.

Key teaching points

  • Emotional safety is built through repeated safe experiences over time.
  • Repair after conflict matters deeply.
  • Consistency matters more than perfect responses.

Group discussion questions

  • What helps children feel emotionally safe in everyday family life?
  • What small routines or repair practices can strengthen trust over time?

Simple activity

Have participants identify one daily routine where they can add more predictability or connection.

Take-home practice

Choose one small change this week that helps your child feel safer and more connected.

Closing reflection

What small act of consistency or repair can I practice this week?
